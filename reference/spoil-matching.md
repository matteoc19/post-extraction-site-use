# Sub-procedure: Regional Spoil-Matching

**Invoke only if the fill/reclamation branch survived Pass 01 — specifically Gate 3 (negative claim), Gate 4 (red team), and Step 5 (existing pathway check) — and was carried forward as the Pass 01 output candidate.**

This sub-procedure *is* Pass 02 for the fill branch. It builds the scenario. It does not validate it — that is Pass 03's job, and Pass 03 will test everything below against statute and the counterparty's actual position.

If a jurisdictional fill-permit market already exists and functions (California IDEFO, Illinois CCDD, equivalents elsewhere), the useful question is *not* "can we match spoil to this site" — brokers already do that. The useful question is narrower: **is there a specific, large, timeline-bounded regional excavation whose spoil is not yet routed, that this site could capture, and that no existing party has connected because the two sides sit in different institutions?**

That is a real but much smaller claim than "we discovered a coordination gap."

## Step A — Establish the fill need precisely  *(Pass 02, Step 6)*

From the site's own governing document, not coverage of it:
- Total volume required
- Split between on-site-generated material and required import (these are different numbers; do not conflate)
- Design surface / end grade, **and the reason for it** (often hydrological or structural — this detail materially strengthens credibility)
- Timeline, including any early phase that is shorter and higher-intensity than the full program

Tag every figure per Gate 2.

## Step B — Identify candidate excavation sources  *(Pass 02, Step 8)*

Look for capital projects with:
- **Timeline overlap** with the site's fill-receiving window
- **Geographic proximity** — haul distance drives both cost and the environmental argument
- **Unresolved disposal** — check environmental review documents for language admitting the spoil destination is undetermined

Caution: in a jurisdiction with a functioning fill market, "destination unresolved" means only that the contractor hasn't selected a vendor yet. It is *not* evidence of a coordination gap. Treat it as leverage only if Step 5 established that no routine market would otherwise capture it.

Categories: transit tunnels and stations, rail grade separations, highway cuts and interchanges, major building foundations, utility trenching, water infrastructure.

## Step C — Derive volumes; show the work  *(Pass 02, Step 7)*

Most projects don't publish spoil volumes. Derive from public dimensions:

- **Tunnels:** cross-sectional area (π × r²) × length ÷ 27 → bank cubic yards. Apply swell factor (1.15–1.35 depending on soil; clay swells more than sand/gravel) → loose/hauled volume. State the factor and its basis.
- **Station boxes / trenches:** L × W × D ÷ 27, same swell conversion.
- **Grade separations:** typological ranges only — pedestrian undercrossing ≈ 100–150k yd³; full vehicular depression ≈ 200–300k yd³. **These are ASSUMED, not DERIVED.** Tag and display accordingly.

A derived number and a typological guess must never look alike in an output.

## Step D — Stage supply against demand in time  *(Pass 02, Step 8)*

Do not compare total supply to total demand. Both are phased.

- Break the site's demand into phases
- Place each source in its own construction window (verify actual schedules — do not assume a project is underway)
- Show where windows overlap and where they gap

**State supply gaps honestly.** If identified sources cover only a fraction of the need, that is often the most important finding: known supply is real but insufficient, which argues *for* deliberate coordination rather than assuming opportunistic capture will suffice.

## Step E — Find the institutional match  *(Pass 02, Step 8 — but see the warning below)*

- A person or body with authority over *both* sides — e.g. a board member sitting on the agency generating the spoil with jurisdiction touching the receiving site. This is a lucky find, not a reliable one; if no such person exists, say so rather than manufacturing a weaker equivalent.
- An **existing** procedural pathway the site has already committed to (a soil management plan, a permit process) that the match can route into — dramatically lower activation energy than proposing a new mechanism.
- The **minimal specific ask** — usually "direct staff to evaluate/engage X," not new policy.

## Known failure modes of this sub-procedure

- **Assuming a coordination gap that a statute already closed.** Check the jurisdiction's fill-permit regime first. This has burned this procedure before.
- **Assuming the void should be filled at all.** Handled upstream at Gate/Step 3 — but if you arrived here without passing it, go back.
- **Assuming fill is scarce.** In some regions the constraint runs the other way: more spoil than places to put it. Establish which side of the market is actually tight before writing a proposal premised on scarcity.
- **Treating a lucky institutional find as a repeatable step.** Most sites have no dual-authority official.
